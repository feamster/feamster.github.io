Securing the Internet's "Data Plane"

Sponsor

Prof. Nick Feamster
feamster@cc.gatech.edu
Area Networking

Problem

Internet routing depends on the cooperation of thousands of competing networks called autonomous systems (ASes), each of which sets policy about the routes it is willing to accept and propagate. Routing security has two angles: (1) control-plane security, which concerns protecting the authenticity of the information carried by the routing protocol itself; and (2) data-plane security, which involves providing guarantees about where traffic actually goes (i.e., whether the path it takes corresponds to the advertised route).

Control-plane security has been studied rather extensively, although all practical solutions to this problem require the deployment of a centralized public-key infrastructure or trusted third party. Data-plane security, however, is an important (and unstudied) problem. This project involves designing new ways to allow hosts on the Internet to gain assurances about the paths their packets actually travel en route to other hosts. Can you augment today's infrastructure to provide support for a host to prevent its packets from traveling through a certain AS? What changes could allow a host to get a receipt that proved its traffic traveled along a certain path? Can you find solutions to these problems that are efficient enough to work at real Internet traffic rates?

Background

As mentioned, there is not a lot of work on data-plane security, although parts of these two papers talk about the problem:

  • L. Subramanian, V. Roth, I. Stoica, S. Shenker, R. Katz
    Listen and Whisper: Security Mechanisms for BGP.
    Usenix NSDI, October 2004.

  • N. Feamster, H. Balakrishnan, J. Rexford
    Some Foundational Problems in Interdomain Routing.
    Proc. ACM SIGCOMM Workshop on Hot Topics in Networking (HotNets-III), November 2004.
To understand the control-plane security problem, and the state of the art, see the following paper:
  • S. Kent, C. Lynn, K. Seo
    Secure Border Gateway Protocol.
    IEEE Journal on Selected Areas in Communication, April 2000
